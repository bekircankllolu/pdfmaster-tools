How to Convert Word to PDF: Quick & Easy Guide

Whether you're sending a resume, submitting a report, or sharing a contract, converting Word to PDF ensures your document looks the same on every device. This guide covers the best methods and tips for a perfect conversion.

Why Convert Word to PDF?

  • Preserved formatting -- Fonts, tables, images, and layouts remain intact regardless of the recipient's software.
  • Professional appearance -- PDF is the standard format for business proposals, contracts, and reports.
  • Edit protection -- Recipients can't accidentally modify your document.
  • Smaller file size -- Image-heavy Word files often compress well when converted to PDF.
  • Universal compatibility -- PDF opens on any OS or device without requiring Microsoft Word.

Methods for Converting Word to PDF

1. Using Microsoft Word

"File > Save As > PDF" works but requires a Word license. Complex formatting (custom fonts, charts) may not transfer perfectly, and it's limited on mobile devices.

2. Server-Based Online Tools

Services like Smallpdf or iLovePDF upload your file to their servers for processing. Convenient, but risky for confidential documents like salary sheets, contracts, or medical records.

3. Browser-Based Conversion (Most Secure)

PDFMASTER Word to PDF processes everything in your browser using JavaScript. Your file never leaves your device. No installation, no account, no payment required.

Tips for Preserving Formatting

  • Use standard fonts -- Arial, Times New Roman, and Calibri render correctly everywhere.
  • Embed images -- Use embedded rather than linked images.
  • Check margins -- Tables and charts may overflow narrow margins.
  • Add manual page breaks -- Automatic breaks may land differently across systems.
  • Review after conversion -- Always open the PDF to verify everything looks correct.

Common Use Cases

  • Job applications -- Most employers prefer resumes in PDF format.
  • Contracts -- Documents meant for signing should be shared as PDF.
  • Academic submissions -- Universities typically accept assignments and theses as PDF.
  • Email attachments -- PDFs open reliably in every email client.
